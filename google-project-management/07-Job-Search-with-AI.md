# 7. Job Search & Interview Prep – Interview Prep

Focused on **behavioral and PM-themed interview prep**: STAR method, sample stories (conflict, failure, leadership, delivery), and common prompts. Use this to structure answers and practice before PM or delivery/consulting interviews.

**Reference:** [CURRICULUM.md](CURRICULUM.md) – Course 7 (job search with AI). This doc emphasizes STAR and behavioral content, not tool walkthroughs.

---

## STAR method

Use STAR to structure behavioral answers so you give a full, concise story.

| Letter | Meaning | What to say (1–2 min total) |
|--------|---------|------------------------------|
| **S** | **Situation** | Brief context: project/team, goal, constraint (time, scope, ambiguity). |
| **T** | **Task** | Your role and what you were responsible for; the challenge or decision. |
| **A** | **Action** | What you did: steps, who you involved, how you communicated, trade-offs. Be specific. |
| **R** | **Result** | Outcome: what shipped, what improved, what you learned. Quantify if possible. |

**Tip:** Lead with the result in one sentence if they want a short answer (“We delivered on time by re-scoping with the sponsor and adding a buffer for the critical path”), then fill in S–T–A if they ask for detail.

---

## STAR template (fill and rehearse)

- **Situation:** [Project/team; goal; key constraint—e.g. “Six-month initiative to launch X; we had a fixed go-live and one key dependency on another team.”]
- **Task:** [Your role; what you had to achieve or fix—e.g. “I had to keep us on track when the dependency slipped.”]
- **Action:** [What you did—e.g. “I ran impact analysis, proposed a scope trim that kept the date, got sponsor sign-off, and re-baselined the plan. I updated stakeholders and ran a retro so we improved dependency tracking.”]
- **Result:** [Outcome and learning—e.g. “We launched on the committed date with the revised scope. We added dependency checks to our planning template for the next project.”]

## Sample STAR stories (PM angle)

### 1. Conflict or disagreement

**Prompt:** “Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a stakeholder or team member.”

- **S:** Cross-functional project; engineering wanted to cut scope to hit the date; product wanted the full feature set. Tension in planning meetings.
- **T:** As PM I had to get to a single, agreed plan without damaging the relationship.
- **A:** I set up a working session with clear criteria: must-have for launch vs nice-to-have. I used the charter and success criteria as the frame. We prioritized together and documented the “phase 2” backlog. I made sure both sides could explain the trade-off to their leads.
- **R:** We shipped the must-haves on time. Product and Eng both felt heard; we used the same process on the next release.

### 2. Failed or troubled project

**Prompt:** “Tell me about a project that failed or didn’t go as planned.”

- **S:** A migration project; we discovered mid-way that the legacy system had more edge cases than we’d scoped. Timeline and confidence were at risk.
- **T:** I had to be honest with the sponsor and reset expectations while still delivering value.
- **A:** I ran a quick impact assessment and options: slip full date, phased delivery, or reduce scope. I presented options with pros/cons and recommended a phased approach. We re-baselined the plan, updated the risk register, and communicated to stakeholders. We also ran a retro to capture what we’d do differently in discovery next time.
- **R:** We delivered phase 1 on a new, realistic date and completed the rest in phase 2. The sponsor appreciated the transparency. We added a discovery checklist for legacy dependencies on future projects.

### 3. Influence without authority

**Prompt:** “Tell me about a time you had to get something done without direct authority over the people involved.”

- **S:** Initiative that needed design, eng, and ops; I was the PM but didn’t manage any of those teams.
- **T:** I had to get commitment to dates and scope from people who had other priorities.
- **A:** I aligned with each lead on how the project supported their goals, co-created the plan so they had input, and made dependencies and handoffs visible. I used the sponsor to reinforce priorities when needed and gave credit in status updates so contributions were visible.
- **R:** We hit the milestones; the same leads volunteered for the next project. I learned that shared ownership and visibility matter more than authority.

### 4. Tight deadline or pressure

**Prompt:** “Describe a situation where you had to deliver under a tight deadline.”

- **S:** Regulatory deadline; non-negotiable date; scope was still being clarified.
- **T:** I had to lock scope and sequence work so we could hit the date without burning out the team.
- **A:** I worked with the sponsor to freeze must-haves and move the rest to post-deadline. I built the schedule back from the date (critical path), identified the risk areas, and set a short status cadence. We had a clear escalation path and a single backlog so nothing slipped through.
- **R:** We met the deadline with the agreed scope. We documented the “frozen scope” approach for future regulated launches.

## Common behavioral prompts (PM / delivery)

Prepare 1–2 STAR stories that you can slightly adapt for these:

| Prompt | Angle to hit |
|--------|---------------|
| **Tell me about a conflict.** | How you facilitated, used data/goals, and preserved the relationship. |
| **Tell me about a failure or missed goal.** | What went wrong, how you communicated and re-baselined, what you learned. |
| **Tell me about influencing without authority.** | How you aligned on goals, gave credit, and used the sponsor. |
| **Tell me about a tight deadline.** | How you managed scope, critical path, and communication. |
| **Tell me about a difficult stakeholder.** | How you understood their concerns, aligned on outcomes, and kept them informed. |
| **Tell me about improving a process.** | What you changed (e.g. retros, checklist, communication), how you measured, what improved. |
| **Tell me about leading without being the manager.** | How you drove alignment, removed blockers, and shared ownership. |

---

## Quick prep checklist

- [ ] Write down 2–3 STAR stories from your own experience (conflict, failure, influence, deadline).
- [ ] Rehearse each in under 2 minutes; lead with result, then S–T–A.
- [ ] Map your stories to the prompts above so one story can serve 2–3 questions with small tweaks.
- [ ] For “walk me through a project,” use the four-phase structure in [06-Capstone-Real-World.md](06-Capstone-Real-World.md).
- [ ] Prepare 1–2 questions to ask them (e.g. how they run projects, what success looks like in the role, how PM and engineering collaborate).
