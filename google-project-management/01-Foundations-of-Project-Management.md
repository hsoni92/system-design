# 1. Foundations of Project Management – Interview Prep

Covers what project management is, the PM role, the project life cycle, methodologies (Waterfall vs Agile), and how organizational structure affects projects. Essential for “What is PM?” and “How do you approach projects?” in interviews.

**Reference:** [CURRICULUM.md](CURRICULUM.md) – Course 1, Modules 1–4.

---

## Key terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Project** | Temporary effort with a defined goal, scope, and deliverables; has a start and end. |
| **Project Management** | Planning, executing, and closing work to meet goals on time, within budget, and to the right quality. |
| **Project Management Life Cycle (PMLC)** | Four phases: Initiating → Planning → Executing → Closing. |
| **Initiating** | Define the project, goals, and high-level scope; get approval to proceed. |
| **Planning** | Break down work, schedule, budget, risks, and communications. |
| **Executing** | Do the work; manage team, quality, and changes. |
| **Closing** | Hand off deliverables, document lessons learned, release resources. |
| **Waterfall** | Sequential phases; requirements fixed early; good when scope is clear and stable. |
| **Agile** | Iterative, incremental delivery; feedback and change built in; good for uncertainty and changing requirements. |
| **Hybrid** | Mix of Waterfall and Agile (e.g. fixed scope and timeline with iterative delivery inside phases). |
| **Functional structure** | Teams by department (e.g. Engineering, Marketing); PM has limited authority; coordination across functions. |
| **Matrix structure** | People report to both a functional manager and a project manager; shared resources; need strong communication. |
| **Projectized structure** | Teams dedicated to projects; PM has high authority; resources report to the project. |
| **PMO (Project Management Office)** | Central group that sets standards, templates, and sometimes assigns PMs and prioritizes projects. |
| **Change management** | Structured approach to moving people and the organization from current state to future state (adoption, training, communication). |
| **Influence without authority** | Getting things done through persuasion, relationships, and data when you don’t control resources or reporting lines. |

## Worked examples

### PMLC: What happens in each phase (tech example)

**Project:** Launch a new partner API for external developers.

| Phase | Example activities |
|-------|--------------------|
| **Initiating** | Define business case; draft project charter; identify sponsor and key stakeholders; get sign-off to start. |
| **Planning** | WBS for API design, security review, docs, and rollout; schedule and milestones; budget; risk and communication plans. |
| **Executing** | Build API; run security review; write docs; pilot with 2–3 partners; track progress and issues. |
| **Closing** | Hand off to support; publish lessons learned; release team to other work; celebrate. |

### Waterfall vs Agile (when to mention each)

| | Waterfall | Agile |
|---|-----------|--------|
| **Best for** | Clear, stable requirements; regulated or contract-heavy work; physical construction. | Uncertainty; need for fast feedback; changing requirements; software/product. |
| **Requirements** | Defined up front. | Evolve with iterations. |
| **Delivery** | Big release at the end. | Small, frequent releases. |
| **Change** | Costly; change control process. | Expected; built into process. |
| **Interview tip** | “We used Waterfall when scope and compliance were fixed.” | “We used Agile to learn quickly and adapt to feedback.” |

### Organizational structure and the PM

| Structure | PM authority | Typical challenge |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Functional** | Low; must influence across departments. | Getting priority and resources from functional managers. |
| **Matrix** | Medium; shared resources. | Competing priorities and “two bosses”; need clear RACI and escalation. |
| **Projectized** | High; team reports to project. | Resource gaps when project ends; need to plan transitions. |

## Interview Q&A

**What is project management?**  
“Project management is planning, executing, and closing temporary work to deliver specific goals on time, within budget, and to the right quality. It includes scope, schedule, cost, risk, and stakeholder management.”

**What are the main phases of a project?**  
“The four phases are Initiating—defining the project and getting approval; Planning—breaking down work, schedule, budget, and risks; Executing—doing the work and managing the team; and Closing—handing off, documenting lessons learned, and releasing resources.”

**How do you choose between Waterfall and Agile?**  
“When requirements are clear and stable, or when contracts or compliance demand fixed scope and phases, I lean toward Waterfall. When there’s uncertainty or we need fast feedback and flexibility, I use Agile or a hybrid so we can deliver in small increments and adapt.”

**What is influence without authority?**  
“It’s getting outcomes when you don’t control people’s time or reporting. I do it by building trust, aligning on goals, using data to make the case, and involving the right people in decisions so they feel ownership.”

**How does organizational structure affect your work as a PM?**  
“In a functional org I spend more time aligning with department leads for resources and priorities. In a matrix I make sure RACI and priorities are clear so people aren’t pulled in conflicting directions. In projectized I have more direct control but also responsibility for team transition when the project ends.”

**What is the role of a PMO?**  
“A PMO sets standards, templates, and sometimes governance. It can provide PMs, prioritize projects, and help share lessons learned. I use the PMO for consistency and for escalation when projects compete for resources.”

## “How would you…” / Behavioral prompts

1. **How would you get a cross-functional team to commit to a project when you don’t manage any of them?**  
   Focus on: clarifying shared goals, involving them in planning, using data to show impact, and getting sponsor support for priorities and conflicts.

2. **How would you explain to a stakeholder why we’re using Agile instead of a single big launch?**  
   Focus on: faster feedback, lower risk of building the wrong thing, ability to adapt to change, and delivering value in smaller steps.

3. **Tell me about a time the organization’s structure made your job harder. What did you do?**  
   Use STAR: situation (e.g. matrix, two bosses), task (deliver on time), action (RACI, escalation path, sponsor alignment), result (delivered or learned).
