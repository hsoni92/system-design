# 2. Project Initiation – Interview Prep

Covers how to start a project: goals (SMART, OKRs), scope, success criteria, stakeholder analysis, RACI, and the project charter. Interviewers often ask how you define success and align stakeholders.

**Reference:** [CURRICULUM.md](CURRICULUM.md) – Course 2, Modules 1–4.

---

## Key terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Project initiation** | Phase where you define the project, benefits, costs, goals, scope, and stakeholders and get approval to proceed. |
| **SMART goals** | Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound. Makes goals clear and testable. |
| **OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)** | Objective = qualitative goal; Key Results = 2–5 measurable outcomes. Used for alignment and tracking (e.g. quarterly). |
| **Scope** | What is in and out of the project; defines boundaries to avoid scope creep. |
| **Scope creep** | Uncontrolled growth of scope without matching time/budget; managed via change control. |
| **Triple constraint** | Scope, time, and cost; changing one affects the others; used to frame trade-offs. |
| **Success criteria** | Measurable conditions that define “done” and project success. |
| **Stakeholder** | Anyone affected by or able to affect the project (sponsor, users, team, execs). |
| **Stakeholder analysis** | Identifying stakeholders and assessing their interest, influence, and impact. |
| **Power/interest grid** | Matrix: high/low power × high/low interest; used to decide engagement (manage closely, keep informed, etc.). |
| **RACI** | Responsible (does the work), Accountable (owns outcome), Consulted (input), Informed (kept in loop). One A per activity. |
| **Project charter** | Authorizing document: project purpose, goals, scope, high-level risks, key stakeholders, sponsor. Gives PM authority. |

## Worked examples

### SMART goals

**Project Plant Pals (example from curriculum):**  
“By end of Q2, deliver 500 plant subscription boxes per month at under 2% defect rate, within the approved budget.”

| Criterion | How it’s met |
|-----------|--------------|
| Specific | 500 boxes/month, subscription product, defect rate. |
| Measurable | 500, 2%, budget. |
| Achievable | Scoped to capacity and quality targets. |
| Relevant | Tied to business growth and quality. |
| Time-bound | By end of Q2. |

**Tech example – Partner API launch:**  
“By the release date, the new partner API will achieve 99.9% uptime and p95 latency under 200 ms, with documentation and runbooks handed to support.”

### OKRs (short example)

- **Objective:** Reliable, fast partner API that developers trust.  
- **Key results:** (1) 99.9% uptime in first quarter post-launch; (2) p95 latency &lt; 200 ms; (3) 20+ partners integrated by month 3.

### Power/interest grid (stakeholder engagement)

| | Low interest | High interest |
|---|----------------|----------------|
| **High power** | Keep satisfied (e.g. exec sponsor) | Manage closely (e.g. sponsor, key client) |
| **Low power** | Monitor | Keep informed (e.g. end users, support) |

*Use:* High power + high interest = frequent, deep engagement. Low power + low interest = minimal updates.

### RACI example (project initiation activities)

| Activity | Sponsor | PM | Tech lead | Marketing |
|----------|---------|-----|-----------|-----------|
| Approve charter | I | C | C | C |
| Define scope | A | R | C | C |
| Stakeholder list | I | R | C | C |
| Sign-off on goals | A | R | C | I |

R = Responsible, A = Accountable, C = Consulted, I = Informed. One A per row.

### Project charter – section checklist

- **Project title and purpose** – Why we’re doing this.
- **Goals / success criteria** – SMART or OKR-style; measurable.
- **Scope (in/out)** – What’s in scope; explicit out-of-scope to prevent creep.
- **Key stakeholders** – Sponsor, PM, key roles; RACI or summary.
- **High-level risks and assumptions** – 2–3 top risks; key assumptions.
- **Approval** – Sponsor (and optionally others) sign-off.

## Interview Q&A

**How do you define project success?**  
“I use SMART goals or OKRs so success is measurable—e.g. delivery date, quality metric, budget. I also define success criteria with the sponsor and document them in the charter so we have a single source of truth.”

**How do you prevent scope creep?**  
“I document in-scope and out-of-scope in the charter and refer back to it. Changes go through a change process: impact on time, cost, quality, and only then approval. I make trade-offs explicit using the triple constraint.”

**How do you identify and manage stakeholders?**  
“I list everyone affected or who can affect the project, then use a power/interest grid to decide who to manage closely vs keep informed. I align on RACI so everyone knows who’s responsible and accountable for key activities.”

**What’s in a project charter and why does it matter?**  
“The charter has purpose, goals, scope, key stakeholders, and high-level risks. It’s the formal authorization: it gives the PM authority and aligns the sponsor and team on what we’re delivering and why.”

**What’s the difference between SMART and OKRs?**  
“SMART is a checklist for a single goal so it’s specific and measurable. OKRs pair a qualitative objective with measurable key results, often used for alignment at org or team level. I use SMART for project goals and OKRs when we need to tie the project to broader objectives.”

## “How would you…” / Behavioral prompts

1. **How would you get alignment on project goals with a reluctant stakeholder?**  
   Focus on: understanding their concerns, linking goals to their interests, using data or benchmarks, and involving them in defining success criteria so they feel ownership.

2. **Tell me about a time scope creep threatened a project. What did you do?**  
   Use STAR: situation (new requests without process), task (protect timeline/quality), action (change process, document out-of-scope, present trade-offs to sponsor), result (scope controlled, delivered on time or re-baselined with approval).

3. **How would you use a RACI chart on a project with multiple teams?**  
   Focus on: listing key deliverables/activities, assigning one A per activity, clarifying R so there’s no gap, and sharing the RACI with everyone so accountability is clear.
